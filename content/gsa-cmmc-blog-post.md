# GSA Just Quietly Extended CMMC to Civilian Contractors — Here's What You Need to Know

*By Capital Cyber / Cyber Grants Alliance | March 10, 2026*

---

On January 5, 2026, the General Services Administration did something that should have made headlines but didn't. They released a new IT Security Procedural Guide that effectively extends CMMC-style cybersecurity requirements to civilian federal contractors.

If you hold a GSA contract and handle Controlled Unclassified Information (CUI), this applies to you.

## What Changed

GSA's new guide requires contractors to implement security controls from **NIST SP 800-171 Revision 3**, obtain independent third-party assessments, and maintain continuous monitoring of their cybersecurity posture. Sound familiar? It should. It's essentially the same framework the Department of Defense has been rolling out under CMMC for defense contractors.

But there's a catch: **GSA chose a different version of the standard than DoD.**

## The Rev 2 vs Rev 3 Problem

DoD's CMMC program is built on NIST 800-171 **Revision 2** (110 controls across 14 families). GSA is requiring **Revision 3** (97 controls, reorganized into new families with more rigorous assessment objectives).

The differences are not cosmetic. Rev 3 consolidates some controls, eliminates others, and adds entirely new security families. The assessment methodology changed. The documentation requirements changed. Even the way controls are structured changed.

For contractors who work with both DoD and GSA, this means **two separate compliance programs**, assessed by **two different assessor ecosystems** (C3PAOs for CMMC, FedRAMP 3PAOs for GSA), built on **two different versions** of the same NIST publication.

Industry leaders have not been quiet about this. Trey Hodgkins, chairman of the cybersecurity division at the National Defense Industrial Association, called it "shocking" that GSA did not leverage the existing CMMC assessor ecosystem. Eric Crusius, a partner at Hunton Andrews Kurth, warned that GSA risks "losing a lot of contractors" if implementation moves too fast.

## The 9 "Showstoppers"

GSA's guide identifies nine security requirements from NIST 800-171 Rev 3 that must be satisfied before a contractor can even receive CUI. These are non-negotiable. They include:

- Access control fundamentals
- Multifactor authentication
- Configuration management
- Vulnerability monitoring
- Boundary protection
- Administrative access controls
- End-of-life risk mitigation

For controls that aren't fully implemented yet, GSA allows a Plan of Action and Milestones (POA&M), but the showstoppers must be in place first.

## One Hour Incident Reporting

Here's a detail that caught our attention: GSA requires incident notification within **one hour** of a suspected or actual cyber incident. Compare that to DoD's 72-hour window under CMMC. That's a significant operational difference that requires a mature incident response capability, not just a policy document in a binder.

## What This Means for the Market

The addressable market for cybersecurity compliance services just expanded dramatically. Until now, CMMC compliance was primarily a defense industry conversation. GSA's move signals that civilian agencies are heading the same direction. NASA, DHS, and others are expected to follow.

For small and mid-size contractors, the compliance burden just doubled. Many civilian contractors who never worried about CMMC are now discovering they need the same level of cybersecurity maturity, but against a different (and in some ways harder) standard.

## What You Should Do Now

**If you're a GSA contractor handling CUI:**

1. **Assess your current posture.** Start with the 9 showstopper controls. If you can't meet those, you can't receive CUI under the new guide.

2. **Understand which version applies to you.** If you do business with both DoD and GSA, you need to map your controls to both Rev 2 and Rev 3. They are not interchangeable.

3. **Get a gap assessment.** The Cyber Grants Alliance offers in-kind $5,000 CMMC Gap Assessments to help contractors understand where they stand before spending on remediation. We're expanding this to cover Rev 3 for GSA contractors as well.

4. **Build your documentation.** GSA expects a System Security and Privacy Plan (SSPP), Privacy Threshold Assessment, supply chain risk management documentation, and continuous monitoring evidence. If you only have a basic CMMC SSP, it likely won't satisfy GSA's requirements.

5. **Plan for dual compliance.** If you serve both DoD and civilian agencies, invest in a unified compliance program that maps to both Rev 2 and Rev 3. This avoids maintaining two completely separate programs.

## The Bottom Line

GSA's quiet January release was anything but routine. It's the first major civilian agency to formally require NIST 800-171 Rev 3 compliance with independent assessments. The era of self-attestation for civilian contractors handling CUI is over.
