## EXECUTIVE SUMMARY

Capital Cyber's website is well built for dental and CPA verticals but **invisible to manufacturing and construction contractors seeking CMMC compliance**. The site mentions "government contractors" as an afterthought rather than a primary audience. Meanwhile, competitors like Summit7, E-N Computers, Stratify IT, and KAMIND all have dedicated industry pages, manufacturing-specific FAQ content, and case studies that directly address the concerns of shop floor operators, fabricators, and construction firms handling CUI.

**The opportunity is massive and time-sensitive.** CMMC Phase 1 enforcement began November 2025. Most small manufacturers and construction subs still aren't compliant. None of the respondents in a recent Cybersecurity Dive survey reported full SPRS scores of 110. There are fewer than 60 C3PAOs for 30,000+ companies. The demand far exceeds supply. Capital Cyber is positioned in Northern Virginia (the densest defense corridor in the country) but the website doesn't capitalize on that geography or the manufacturing/construction audience.

**Bottom line:** With targeted content, two new landing pages, and a messaging pivot, Capital Cyber can capture manufacturing/construction CMMC leads within 30 days.

## PART 3: MANUFACTURING & CONSTRUCTION PAIN POINTS

### 3.1 Manufacturing CMMC Challenges (From Research)

These are the specific problems manufacturers face that Capital Cyber must address on its website:

1. **Legacy Systems & Industrial Control Systems (ICS):** Most manufacturing floors run CNC machines, PLCs, and SCADA systems that weren't built with cybersecurity in mind. These can't be easily patched or updated. Compensating controls are required.

2. **CUI on the Shop Floor:** Engineering drawings, specifications, part numbers, and technical data packages (TDP) are CUI. They flow through CAD stations, ERP systems, email, and sometimes paper printouts on the shop floor. Scoping where CUI lives is hard.

3. **ERP System Scoping:** If an ERP system (SAP, Epicor, JobBOSS) touches CUI (even indirectly through linked documents), it's in scope for CMMC. Misclassifying ERP is a common audit failure.

4. **CAD/CAM Software in Secure Enclaves:** AutoCAD, SolidWorks, Mastercam, and similar tools need to run inside CMMC-compliant environments. This often means Azure Virtual Desktop with GPU acceleration in GCC High. It's complex and expensive.

5. **ITAR Overlap:** Many defense manufacturers handle ITAR-controlled data. ITAR requires U.S. persons only access. CMMC + ITAR together create compounding compliance requirements. Violations carry criminal penalties.

6. **Subcontractor Flow-Down:** If a manufacturer shares CUI with a heat treater, plating shop, or any sub, that sub must also be CMMC certified at the same level. Most small subs don't know this.

7. **Multi-Entity Compliance:** Acquisitions and mergers create multi-entity structures where both companies need separate CMMC certification. This is extremely common in manufacturing.

8. **Resource Constraints:** Small manufacturers (20 to 100 employees) don't have IT departments. They can't afford full-time cybersecurity staff. They need an MSP/MSSP that understands manufacturing.

9. **SPRS Scores:** No surveyed manufacturers reported a score of 110 (required for full compliance). 17% still had negative scores. This is a crisis.

10. **Cost:** CMMC Level 2 compliance costs $75,000 to $200,000 for a small business. Assessment fees alone run $30,000 to $70,000. Manufacturers need to budget this into overhead.

### 3.2 Construction CMMC Challenges (From Research)

1. **Project-Based IT:** Construction companies set up temporary offices at job sites with varying levels of IT infrastructure. Securing CUI across multiple, temporary locations is fundamentally different from securing a fixed office.

2. **Subcontractor Sprawl:** A typical DoD construction project involves dozens of subcontractors. CUI flow-down requirements apply to every sub that touches sensitive data. Managing compliance across a fragmented supply chain is the #1 construction-specific challenge.

3. **Legacy Systems and Paper Processes:** Construction lags behind other sectors in cybersecurity adoption (per the American Bar Association). Many firms still rely on paper plans, USB drives, and unsecured file sharing.

4. **Public Works Exposure:** Roads, bridges, military bases, and public buildings funded by federal/local programs all fall under DoD contracting requirements. Construction companies that never thought of themselves as "defense contractors" are suddenly in scope.

5. **Documentation Culture Gap:** Construction firms aren't used to creating and maintaining cybersecurity policies, procedures, and evidence of implementation. This organizational maturity gap is even wider than in manufacturing.

6. **Ongoing Compliance:** CMMC isn't a one-time certification. Construction companies must continuously monitor systems, update practices, and maintain audit readiness through their 3-year certification cycle.

7. **Surety/Bonding Implications:** Construction companies that lose DoD eligibility due to CMMC non-compliance may also face bonding issues, since bonding companies evaluate financial stability and contract pipeline.
