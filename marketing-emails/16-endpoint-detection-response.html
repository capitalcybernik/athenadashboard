If you're still relying on traditional antivirus, there's something you should know: it's not enough anymore. It hasn't been for a while.

Traditional antivirus works by matching files against a list of known threats. If the malware is on the list, it gets blocked. If it's new, modified, or uses a fileless technique, it walks right past. And most modern attacks are designed to do exactly that.

Endpoint Detection and Response (EDR) works differently. Instead of just matching signatures, it watches what programs actually do on your computer. If something starts encrypting files, if a script tries to disable your security tools, if a process reaches out to a known command and control server, EDR catches it based on behavior. It doesn't need to have seen that exact malware before.

When EDR detects something malicious, it doesn't just send an alert and hope someone reads it. It can automatically isolate the device from the network, kill the malicious process, and roll back any changes. That's the difference between "we got an alert" and "we stopped the attack."

Every cyber insurance carrier now requires EDR. Not antivirus. EDR. If your application says "antivirus" where it should say "endpoint detection and response," that's a gap that could cost you coverage.
