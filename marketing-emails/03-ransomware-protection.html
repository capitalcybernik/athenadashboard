If you have cyber liability insurance (and you should), your carrier has a list of security controls they expect you to have in place. Ransomware protection is at the top of that list.

Here's why that matters: if you get hit with ransomware and you don't have the right protections in place, your insurance company can deny the claim. It's happening more and more. Carriers are done paying out for businesses that didn't do the basics.

I've seen this firsthand. Years ago, the company I was working for got hit with a ransomware attack. $500,000 in ransom. The insurance company paid out $700,000 because we had done things right. Today? Those same carriers would look at your security posture with a microscope before writing you a check.

The controls they're looking for aren't complicated, but they do need to be in place and properly configured:

Endpoint detection and response on every device.

Immutable backups that ransomware can't encrypt or delete.

Multi-factor authentication on email and remote access.

Email filtering that catches malicious attachments and links.

Incident response planning so your team knows what to do when something happens.

We set all of this up for our clients. When renewal time comes around and the carrier sends that security questionnaire, our clients can check every box honestly. That translates directly into lower premiums and actual coverage when it counts.
